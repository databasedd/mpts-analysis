The Headline Trap

Imagine you read: "Group A accounts for 60% of all tribunal cases!"

This sounds alarming. But is it? That depends entirely on how many people are in Group A to begin with.

Misleading Stat

60%
"Group A is 60% of cases"

Missing Context

75%
"Group A is 75% of the workforce"

Key insight: If Group A makes up 75% of the workforce but only 60% of tribunal cases, they're actually underrepresented in tribunals â€” the opposite of what the headline implies.

The Formula

We calculate the representation ratio like this:

Representation Ratio = % of tribunal cases % of workforce
Ratio = 1.0 means proportional | >1.0 means overrepresented | <1.0 means underrepresented

Real World Application

In our MPTS data, we compare tribunal appearance rates against the GMC workforce baseline â€” the actual ethnic composition of UK doctors.

For example:

  • British doctors might be the majority of tribunal cases in absolute numbers, but if they're also the majority of the workforce, they could be proportionally represented or even underrepresented.
  • A smaller ethnic group might have fewer total cases, but if those cases represent a higher percentage than their workforce share, they're overrepresented.

This is why we show both views: The raw numbers tell you the scale. The representation ratio tells you if there's a disparity worth investigating.

Toggle "Show Over/Under Representation" on the dashboard to see both perspectives.

Important Caveats

Representation ratios reveal patterns, not causes. Overrepresentation could result from:

  • Actual differences in behavior or practice patterns
  • Differences in specialties, career stages, or practice settings
  • Bias in referral, investigation, or adjudication processes
  • Statistical noise in small samples
  • Confounding factors we haven't measured

Correlation is not causation. This data identifies disparities that warrant further investigation â€” it does not prove discrimination or exonerate any group.
